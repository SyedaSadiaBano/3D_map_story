The Rising Tide: Visualizing Sea-Level Rise on the Sindh Coast

A projection of coastal flood risk for 2030, 2040, and 2050 based on IPCC AR6 data.

Introduction: A Coastline at Risk

The coastline of Sindh, a densely populated and economically vital region, faces a significant threat from global sea-level rise. This story map visualizes the progressive expansion of areas that will become vulnerable to episodic coastal flooding over the next three decades. The projections are based on a moderate emissions scenario (SSP2-4.5) from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC).

2030: The Baseline Risk

By 2030, areas at or near the current mean sea level (0 meters elevation) are considered to be at high risk of permanent inundation or frequent tidal flooding. This represents the immediate, baseline risk that coastal communities face.

2040: Expanding Vulnerability

As the sea level continues to rise, the zone of vulnerability expands inland. The area within 50 meters of the baseline inundation zone becomes highly susceptible to storm surges and extreme high tides. This represents a growing threat to coastal infrastructure and ecosystems.

2050: A Critical Threshold

By mid-century, the flood risk zone expands significantly. The area within 150 meters of the baseline becomes the new frontier of coastal risk. This scenario highlights the urgent need for long-term adaptation and planning to protect coastal communities and assets from the compounding effects of a rising sea.

Conclusion

This visualization demonstrates the clear and progressive nature of the threat posed by sea-level rise. While the changes in the coming decades may seem incremental, they represent a significant increase in risk for the people, economy, and environment of the Sindh coast. Proactive planning and adaptation measures are essential to build resilience against this rising tide.
